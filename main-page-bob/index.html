The anatomy of anxiety

Anatomy of anxiety explores the topic of anxiety in a journey that will help you, be the best version of you. To begin this journey, we invite you to meet Bob and learn about the physical signs of anxiety.

Meet Bob


He is just like me and you. And he suffers from anxiety too. To have anxiety means to be human. Explore the various parts of Bob that exhibit anxiety and learn more about him and maybe even you.
your mind is your greatest ally or your greatest enemy... anxiety can have many impacts on your mind which include: excessive fear and worry, catatrophising and obsessive thinking. Have you ever felt an uneasy and sick feeling down in the pit of your stomach? that is usually a sign of anxiety. oftentimes your legs can shake and bounce when your anxious as a form of fidgeting or moving. whilst not exactly physical, your heart rate can spike if you feel anxious. An increase in heart rate can lead to other physical symptoms such as sweating or shaking. anxiety can create shortness in breath or increase breathing rate. sometimes you will notice someone appear breathless or shallow in their breathing, this can be due to anxiety. anxiety oftentimes causes sweating of the palms!

recognising anxiety


recognising anxiety in yourself

Recognising anxiety in yourself is a tricky task but requires mindfullness. The best things to look out for include:

- Catastrophising: things are not as bad as they sometimes

- Overthinking: your worst enemy, what will be will be my friend.

- Fidgeting: we tend to fidget a lot when anxious, it can be as simple as playing around with your own fingers

- Concentration: our concentration can lack if we are feeling anxious

recognising anxiety in others

Recognising anxiety in others may seem difficult at first, but once you know what you're looking for it becomes much easier.

- Bouncing legs: oftentimes when anxious one will excessivly bounce their legs

- Sweating: especially of the palms and face, we often sweat when not feeling so great

- Tiredness: if someone appears as if they are too tired to do anything, this can be because of anxiety

- Quiet: sometimes we reduce ourselves to minimal communication when we feel anxious

coping with anxiety

Helping others can be tricky but it often requires patience and friendly advice.

When helping others, sometimes its best to simply listen to them rather than offer advice. Sometimes lending an ear is all they need. To help yourself often means to recognise you require help first. So often we deny they we have these negative feelings and ignore them.

The best thing to do is to communicate, talk about your feelings. Whether that is to someone you trust like a guardian or friend, or to a professional like a therapist.

On going help can include things like meditating, breathing techniques, recoginsing when your over-thinking and living in the moment. How can we help everyone around us? Simple, be kind. Being kind, respectful and approachable will naturally help those around you feel comfortable enough to talk to you. Even about complicated things like anxiety and mental health problems.

Do not find people to help, but be the lighthouse that stands tall and is a beacon of hope and helpfullness. Being kind and approachable can go a long way. Others

stigma around anxiety

The stigma surrounding Mental Health in Australia is poor. It reduces a major problem to a "she'll be right" attitude that encourages men and women to disregard their emotions and move forward with no help or thought. Whilst it is getting better, stigma only reduces the dialogue between each other in the fear of looking weak. ALWAYS remember, it is okay to not be okay.
